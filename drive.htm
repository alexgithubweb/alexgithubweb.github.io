Driving techniques

Starting your engine

Let's start with the beginning. The GT Turbo, as most carburettor cars, is a bit tricky to be started (if you come from injection cars or from no car at all). There are two basic procedures:

  • If the engine is cold, it depends on the weather:
    • Very cold weather. Pull the choke lever all the way. Don't accelerate. It should start easily, so just take care and try to keep the rpm level as low as you can until the oil reaches all the engine points.
    • Cold weather. Pull the choke lever half the way (experience and your own choke cable tension will give you the right point). Don't accelerate.
    • Warm weather. Don't accelerate and turn the starter motor. When it looks like it will start, leave the contact key and use the gas pedal slightly, with little pushes, to keep the engine alive at a low rpm level.
    • Hot weather. Same as before, but it'll be easier to make it go
  • If the engine is hot, it depends on how long the engine was switched off:
    • Very recently (i.e. 1 min.). Just make it turn and it'll work.
    • Recently (i.e. 5 min.). Push the gas pedal slightly (1/5 of the career) and keep it pushed while you use the starter motor. When you sense it lives, stop accelerating to avoid high engine speeds.
    • A while ago (i.e. 15 min.). The antipercolation system will have to work to let you go home. Push the gas pedal almost all the way (3/4) and act as before.
    • Long ago (i.e. 30 min.). Same as switched off recently, the overheating of being stopped will have had time to go away.
    • Very long ago (i.e. 4 hours) Then the engine is cold, see above! :-)

Double clutching

When changing gears, the gear lever connects the selected speed mainshaft (or secondary shaft) gear, always engaged to the input (or primary) shaft correlated gear to the mainshaft itself. The mainshaft is rotating at a proportional speed to the wheels one, while the mainshaft gear rotates with the engine speed, at the moment of the clutch pedal stepping, and decreasing because of the oil and bearing friction.

Gearing up isn't usually a problem, since the synchro gear engagement system can handle the minor spinning speed differences. The input shaft decreases its speed until being close to the mainshaft one (and the engine will move to a lower r.p.m. after the gear change). Meanwhile, gearing down is much more difficult, since the input shaft should rotate faster (you'll get into more r.p.m. after the gear change) than it was, which is not possible. That's why it's harder to engage a lower gear, it requires more time and it punishes the gearbox shortening the synchro rings life.

The solution is to use the double-clutch manoeuvre. The principle is to match the shaft and gear speed before engaging them via adjusting the primary (and so gear) speed with right engine speed in neutral. That's made trying to rev the engine just to the point it'll be in the gear you're going. This is the tricky part, but it's a matter of practice.

It can be applied to gearing up or down:

  • To gear up, you should just rev slightly the input shaft, as follows.

      Initially, you are accelerating in one speed (1st in the figure).
      Press the clutch as usual, but get the lever into neutral.
      Release the clutch keeping the lever in neutral. Give one (or two) fast kick(s) to the gas pedal up to give the right speed to the input shaft. This is the tricky part, but it's a matter of practice.
      Press the clutch pedal again and engage the next speed.
      As usual in any gear change, release the clutch pedal and start accelerating again.

  • To gear down, you'll need to speed up the input shaft much more. The braking you obtain with the gearing down won't be that violent, and even if you speed it up too much, the car won't tend to brake immediately, but the gear engagement will be much cleaner and fast so the reduction will be faster and more effective. In addition, as you're reducing the car's speed, it's logical to brake at the same time. This requires to the tip-heel foot movement: Using the heel or the foot side to accelerate while braking with the tip, as follows:

      Initially, you are braking in one speed (3rd in the figure).
      Press the clutch, continue braking and get the lever into neutral.
      Release the clutch keeping the lever in neutral. Keep on braking. Give one (or two) fast kick(s) to the gas pedal up to give the right speed to the input shaft.
      Always braking, press the clutch pedal again and engage the previous speed.
      Release the clutch pedal. Continue braking.

With a good ear to adjust the revs, you can feel how easily the gear lever engages the speed, and theorically you could drive without even using the clutch. Of course this is only useful if you break the clutch cable or something like that...

Hand-braking in bends

Disclaimer: These sliding techniques are evidently not intended for road use.

A common problem with front-wheel drive cars is that, being all the weight located on the front axis, the car is prone to understeering, specially if you accelerate hard. In sharp bends or in an emergency situation, a controlled slide of the rear axle might be interesting. The hand-brake can be used for that, if you give a fast pull and release at the right point of the bend, when the car weight is located on the outer front wheel. If you're accelerating hard or the bend is not sharp enough the rear end will be too charged and the pull won't achieve anything. Controlling a slide is not trivial, as counter-steering and gas pedal must be carefully and precisely applied to avoid the full turn or a new slide in the opposite direction. Practice in an open lonely surface is a must-to to get the car's reactions.

Counter-steering

When a rear axle slide starts, depending on the speed you have, the car starts turning (brutally) and will tend to turn up to 180°. If is not the desired angle, or even the slide was not desired at all, counter-steering must be applied. That's turning the steering wheel to where the rear is going to make the front advance in that direction too. The wheel must be turned the right amount, and it's a matter of practice because you must learn to 'feel' the front wheels position. Once the slide is being controlled, the steering wheel must be returned without delay to its position to avoid a sliding to the opposite direction that would invariably take you out of the road. A mark in the top part of the steering wheel like those some sport wheels have could be a help, but get the feeling is the most important.

In addition, the pedal manipulation have a great influence on the sliding. If you brake, even slightly, the car weight transfers to the front axle and the rear one losses adherence (more slide) while if you accelerate, the opposite phenomenon appears: less rear slide (and maybe understeering).

Slow steering gears will need a lot of steering wheel turns, which can be difficult to apply in time. The GT Turbo has a relatively fast one, so fast hands and practice in an open free surface is all you should require.
